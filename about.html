About the Archive

The record has always
been incomplete.

Queer history is not missing by accident. It has been destroyed, suppressed, rewritten, and buried — systematically, across centuries, across continents. What we know about queer life throughout history is a fraction of what existed. Marq'd exists to make sure the same is not said of us.

The United States

Safety is not a
given — even here.

It is easy to assume that legal progress in the United States means queer people are safe. The reality is more complicated. There is still no federal law that explicitly protects LGBTQ+ people from housing discrimination. Protections depend on where you live — and in nearly two dozen states, they are absent at the state level entirely.

A 2021 executive order directed the Department of Housing and Urban Development to interpret the Fair Housing Act's sex protections to include sexual orientation and gender identity. That executive order can be reversed. It is not a law. In states without their own explicit protections, LGBTQ+ renters and homebuyers have limited or no legal recourse against discrimination — even when that discrimination is blatant.

28% of the LGBTQ+ population in the United States lives in states that do not prohibit housing discrimination based on sexual orientation or gender identity. In some of those states, local municipalities are preempted from passing their own protections.

What happens when the law says you are protected, but the administration chooses not to enforce it? The answer is: you are not protected.

Marq'd exists partly because legal protection and lived safety are not the same thing. Stories of housing discrimination, workplace hostility, and daily navigations of unsafe space happen in San Diego and San Antonio, not just in places that appear on maps of criminalization. Every geography is a queer geography. Every story belongs in the archive.

State by State

The patchwork of LGBTQ+ housing protection in the U.S.

Explicit protections

23 states + D.C. explicitly prohibit housing discrimination based on sexual orientation and gender identity.

Interpreted only

8 states interpret existing sex discrimination bans to include LGBTQ+ people — but this interpretation is not guaranteed.

No state protection

19 states have passed no state-level protections. Some prevent cities from creating their own ordinances.

Federal floor only

Residents in unprotected states rely solely on federal enforcement — which has shifted under every administration.

What this means in practice

A landlord in an unprotected state can decline to rent to a same-sex couple. A property manager can ask invasive questions about gender identity. A bank can apply different standards to a queer applicant for a mortgage. These things happen. Most go unreported because there is no legal pathway to pursue them.

A History of Erasure

This is not the first time
the record has been cleared.

The destruction of queer history is not a metaphor. It is a documented, recurring pattern — carried out by governments, institutions, families, and even by queer people themselves, acting in fear. What survives is what was hidden well enough, or preserved by people who understood what they were protecting.

The loss is immeasurable precisely because we cannot know what we have lost. The letters that were never written. The diaries burned before their authors died. The photographs never taken because taking them was dangerous. The oral histories that died with their speakers, undocumented and unrecorded.

Every instance of erasure below represents not one lost record, but hundreds or thousands — the ones we know about, standing in for the ones we don't.

The roots of the Archive lie in the silent voices, the love letters destroyed, the pronouns changed, the diaries carefully edited, the pictures never taken.

— Joan Nestle, co-founder, Lesbian Herstory Archives, Brooklyn

Erasure is not only historical. In February 2025, the U.S. federal government began systematically removing references to transgender and queer people from government websites, replacing decades of documented history with absence. The LGBTQ+ History Association called it "part of a much broader pattern of attacks on accurate and diverse history." Archivists and scholars moved immediately to preserve what was being deleted.

The archive is always under threat. That is why it must always be built.

A Partial Timeline
Medieval Europe
Records of sodomy trials were burned alongside those convicted — the evidence and the person destroyed simultaneously. The documents that might have documented queer life were considered too dangerous to keep.
1860
British colonial rule criminalized homosexuality in India under Section 377 — erasing centuries of documented queer existence in Hindu texts and temple art. Most modern Indians came to believe homosexuality was a Western import. It was the criminalization that was imported.
1933
The Nazi regime burned the Institut für Sexualwissenschaft in Berlin — the world's first LGBTQ+ research institute, containing over 12,000 books, 35,000 photographs, and decades of case histories. The famous Nazi book-burning images are, at their core, images of queer history being destroyed.
1940s – 1960s
The Lavender Scare: the U.S. federal government purged thousands of gay and lesbian employees, treating homosexuality as a security threat. Queer people burned their own letters, diaries, and photographs to protect themselves.
Ongoing
Descendants, biographers, and institutional archivists have consistently "straightwashed" historical figures — altering pronouns, removing partners from records, declining to catalog materials. In some archives, queer materials are still kept under restricted access or described with clinical euphemism.
2025
The U.S. federal government orders the removal of LGBTQ+ content from government websites and directs agencies to recognize only two sexes. Historians and archivists scramble to preserve what is being deleted in real time.
Why Marq'd Exists

Primary sources.
Written by us.

A primary source is a firsthand account — a letter, a diary, a testimony, a photograph. Historians rank primary sources as the most valuable form of evidence because they have not been filtered through interpretation. They are the record of something as it was actually experienced.

For most of recorded history, queer people did not create primary sources about their own lives — because doing so was dangerous. When they did, those records were often destroyed by others, or by themselves. What survived was what could be hidden.

Marq'd is built on a simple premise: every story submitted here is a primary source. It is assigned a permanent record number, a geographic coordinate, and a timestamp. It can be cited by historians, journalists, and researchers exactly as any primary document would be cited. It is not curated for palatability. It is not filtered for safety. It is the record of queer life as it was actually lived.

The dots on the globe are not symbolic. Each one is a document. Together, they are an archive that could not have existed at any earlier point in history — one that belongs to no institution, no government, and no individual other than the people who contributed to it.
